### The Cryptographic Vulnerability

Current encryption methods rely on mathematical problems that are computationally difficult for classical computers but trivial for sufficiently powerful quantum computers:

**RSA Encryption**: Vulnerable to Shor's algorithm, which can factor large integers exponentially faster than classical methods.

**Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC)**: Also susceptible to quantum attacks using modified versions of Shor's algorithm.

**Symmetric Encryption**: While more resistant, key lengths must be doubled to maintain security against quantum attacks.

## NIST Post-Quantum Cryptography Standards

The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) has been leading the global effort to standardize quantum-resistant cryptographic algorithms. After years of evaluation, NIST has selected the first set of post-quantum cryptographic standards.

### Selected Algorithms

**CRYSTALS-Kyber**: Selected for general encryption, based on the learning-with-errors problem.

**CRYSTALS-Dilithium**: Chosen for digital signatures, offering strong security guarantees.

**FALCON**: An alternative digital signature algorithm optimized for smaller signature sizes.

**SPHINCS+**: A hash-based signature scheme providing additional security options.

### Implementation Challenges

**Performance Impact**: Post-quantum algorithms typically require larger key sizes and more computational resources.

**Compatibility Issues**: Legacy systems may struggle to accommodate new cryptographic requirements.

**Standardization Timeline**: Organizations need 10-15 years for complete cryptographic migration.

## Quantum Key Distribution (QKD)

Quantum Key Distribution represents a fundamentally different approach to secure communication, using the principles of quantum mechanics to detect eavesdropping attempts.

### How QKD Works

**Quantum Entanglement**: Pairs of photons are created in entangled states and distributed between communicating parties.

**Measurement Detection**: Any attempt to intercept or measure quantum states disturbs them, revealing the presence of an eavesdropper.

**Unconditional Security**: QKD provides information-theoretic security based on the laws of physics rather than computational complexity.

### Current QKD Deployments

**Commercial Networks**: Companies like ID Quantique and Toshiba have deployed QKD systems for banking and government communications.

**Research Networks**: Academic institutions worldwide are building quantum communication testbeds.

**Satellite QKD**: China's Micius satellite has demonstrated quantum communication over 1,200 kilometers.
